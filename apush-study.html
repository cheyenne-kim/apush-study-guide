The Big Picture

Before 1492, the Americas were home to tens of millions of people organized into hundreds of distinct cultures — from the city-states of Mesoamerica to the confederacies of the Northeast to the farming villages of the Southwest. These were not primitive societies; the Aztec capital Tenochtitlán had a population larger than any European city at the time.

Columbus's 1492 voyage unleashed the Columbian Exchange — the most transformative biological event in human history. European diseases (smallpox, measles, typhus) killed an estimated 50–90% of the Native American population, who had zero immunity. This demographic catastrophe reshaped everything: it created a massive labor shortage that eventually drove the Atlantic slave trade, it opened land that Europeans interpreted as "empty" and ripe for colonization, and it fundamentally disrupted Native political and social structures.

Spain built the first major colonial empire, using the encomienda system to extract labor and resources. But Spanish colonization was never monolithic — Franciscan missionaries, African soldiers, and Native allies all shaped the emerging colonial world. Meanwhile, France pursued furs and alliances, and England was just beginning to notice the possibilities across the Atlantic.

Key Vocabulary

Transfer of plants, animals, diseases, and people between the Old and New Worlds after 1492. Bidirectional but devastating for Native Americans.

Encomienda

Spanish labor system granting colonists the right to demand labor/tribute from Native Americans in exchange for "protection" and Christianization. In practice: forced labor.

Syncretism

Blending of different cultural/religious practices. E.g., Native Americans blending Christianity with indigenous spiritual practices under Spanish rule.

Mestizo

Person of mixed European and Native American ancestry. Result of widespread intermarriage/mixing under Spanish colonialism.

European (especially English) narrative emphasizing Spanish colonial cruelty. Originated with Las Casas; used by Protestant nations to contrast their own colonialism as more humane.

Network connecting Europe, Africa, and the Americas: manufactured goods → Africa; enslaved people → Americas; raw materials → Europe.

The Big Picture

By 1607, England was a late arrival to colonization. Spain and Portugal already had empires. But England's colonies, beginning with Jamestown, would develop in a distinctly different direction — shaped more by market forces and voluntary (and involuntary) migration than by the crown's direct control.

The most important story of this period is the transformation of colonial labor systems. Jamestown initially used white indentured servants who worked 4–7 years for passage, then received freedom and (theoretically) land. But Bacon's Rebellion (1676) — a multiracial uprising of poor whites, freed servants, and enslaved people against the Virginia elite — terrified planters. Their solution: replace indentured servants with permanently enslaved Africans, whose children would also be enslaved. By 1700, Virginia law had made slavery hereditary and race-based. This is a pivot point in American history.

Meanwhile, regional diversity was remarkable. New England Puritans built tight, theocratic communities around covenant theology — but dissenters like Roger Williams and Anne Hutchinson were banished. The Middle Colonies (Pennsylvania especially) pioneered religious tolerance. The Great Awakening of the 1730s–40s disrupted all three regions with emotional, democratic preaching that inadvertently taught colonists to challenge authority — useful skills for a revolution.

Key Vocabulary

Headright System

Virginia policy granting 50 acres to anyone who paid for a settler's passage. Attracted indentured servants; also enriched wealthy planters who could import many workers.

Indentured Servitude

Contract labor: worker receives passage to America in exchange for 4–7 years of unpaid labor. Unlike slavery, it was temporary — but conditions were often brutal.

Chattel Slavery

Property-based slavery where enslaved people and their children are legally "chattels" (moveable property). Became race-based in Virginia after 1660s laws.

Covenant Theology

Puritan belief that their community had a special covenant (contract) with God to build a holy society. Basis for New England's communal, theocratic organization.

Mercantilism

Economic theory that colonies exist to enrich the mother country. England's Navigation Acts required colonial goods to flow through English ships and ports.

Halfway Covenant (1662)

Puritan compromise allowing partial church membership to those not fully converted. Showed weakening of original Puritan religious intensity by mid-1600s.

New Light vs. Old Light

Great Awakening split: "New Lights" embraced emotional revivalism; "Old Lights" (established clergy) condemned it as dangerous enthusiasm. Reflects democratization of religion.

Which comparison between New England and Chesapeake colonies in the 17th century is most accurate?
Answer: A
New England's cold climate limited disease; Puritan communities emphasized family and community; life expectancy was near English levels. Chesapeake was a death trap — malaria, dysentery, and the brutal tobacco labor system killed colonists rapidly. The skewed sex ratio (mostly men) meant fewer families. These demographic differences shaped entirely different social structures and explain why slavery took hold faster in the tobacco-growing South.

The Big Picture

The French and Indian War (1754–63) broke the colonial relationship. For 150 years, Britain had practiced salutary neglect — loosely governing the colonies and letting them develop their own assemblies, economies, and identities. The war's massive debt forced Britain to demand the colonies pay their share, shattering this arrangement. The Stamp Act (1765), Townshend Acts (1767), Tea Act (1773) — each new measure met escalating resistance rooted in the Enlightenment argument that taxation without representation was tyranny.

The American Revolution was genuinely radical for its time: it asserted that government derived its legitimacy from the consent of the governed, not divine right. But it was also deeply limited — enslaved people, women, and Native Americans were largely excluded from its promises, even as they participated in the war on all sides.

The new nation's first attempt at governance — the Articles of Confederation — reflected fear of strong central government. But Shays' Rebellion (1786) exposed its fatal weakness: Congress could not tax, could not enforce laws, could not regulate trade. The Constitutional Convention (1787) produced a new framework that has endured, but only after fierce debate between Federalists (Hamilton, Madison) and Anti-Federalists (Patrick Henry, George Mason). The Bill of Rights was the price of ratification.

The 1790s saw the first real test of the new government: Hamilton's financial program, the Whiskey Rebellion, the XYZ Affair, and the Alien and Sedition Acts all revealed that the fundamental question — how much power should the federal government have? — was far from settled.

Key Vocabulary

Virtual vs. Actual Representation

British argued Parliament "virtually" represented all British subjects. Colonists insisted on "actual" representation — only bodies they elected could tax them.

Natural Rights

Locke's theory that all people have inherent rights (life, liberty, property) that governments cannot take away. Foundation of the Declaration of Independence.

Republicanism

Political philosophy holding that citizens should sacrifice personal interests for the common good; representative government; rejection of monarchy and hereditary privilege.

Elastic Clause

"Necessary and proper" clause of the Constitution. Hamilton used it to justify the national bank; Jefferson argued it couldn't be stretched this far. Central to strict/loose construction debate.

Strict vs. Loose Construction

Strict (Jefferson): federal government can only do what Constitution explicitly says. Loose (Hamilton): government can do what's "necessary and proper" for its functions. Ongoing American debate.

Federalist Papers

85 essays by Hamilton, Madison, and Jay defending the Constitution. Key ones: #10 (factions/large republic), #51 (checks and balances). Primary source for constitutional interpretation.

Nullification

Virginia and Kentucky Resolutions (1798) argued states could declare federal laws unconstitutional. Used again during Nullification Crisis (1832) and by Southern states before Civil War.

The Big Picture

Period 4 is defined by two revolutions happening simultaneously: an economic revolution (the Market Revolution) and a political revolution (Jacksonian democracy). Together they transformed American society from a republic of small farmers into an industrial, commercial nation — but one that expanded rights for some while brutally restricting them for others.

The Market Revolution connected the nation through canals, steamboats, and railroads. The Erie Canal (1825) made New York City the commercial capital of the continent. Lowell mills drew young farm women into wage labor. Southern cotton production exploded, making slavery more profitable than ever — and more entrenched. The North industrialized; the South deepened its commitment to slave-based agriculture. This economic divergence was the structural foundation of the Civil War.

Andrew Jackson represents the contradictions of this era. He championed "the common man" — expanding white male suffrage, attacking the Bank as an elite institution — while simultaneously driving Native Americans off their ancestral lands in the Trail of Tears and defending the plantation system. Jacksonian democracy expanded democracy for white men while restricting it for everyone else.

The 1830s–40s also produced America's greatest wave of reform movements, energized by the Second Great Awakening's message that individuals could achieve moral perfection and therefore society could be perfected. The Big Picture

Period 5 is the most dramatic in American history: the nation fought its bloodiest war, abolished slavery, and then failed to secure equality for the people it had freed. Understanding this period requires holding two truths simultaneously — the Civil War did destroy slavery, AND Reconstruction's promise was systematically betrayed.

The crisis accelerated through the 1850s: the Compromise of 1850 (and its Fugitive Slave Act infuriating Northerners), the Kansas-Nebraska Act (reopening the slavery question), Dred Scott (ruling Black people had no citizenship rights), and John Brown's raid all shattered the possibility of peaceful resolution. When Lincoln won in 1860 — without a single Southern electoral vote — Southern states seceded before he even took office.

Lincoln did not go to war to end slavery. His initial goal was preserving the Union. The Emancipation Proclamation (Jan 1, 1863) was a strategic war measure — it reframed the war as a fight against slavery, preventing Britain from recognizing the Confederacy, and authorized Black men to serve in the Union Army. The 13th Amendment (1865) finally abolished slavery nationwide.

Reconstruction (1865–1877) was a genuine attempt to rebuild the South on a foundation of racial equality. The 14th and 15th Amendments, the Freedmen's Bureau, and Black political participation were real achievements. But they depended on federal enforcement. When Northern political will collapsed — tired of the struggle, distracted by economic issues, and as part of the Compromise of 1877 — federal troops withdrew, and "Redeemer" Democrats used violence and law to restore white supremacy.

The Big Picture

The Gilded Age (Mark Twain's term) refers to the era of rapid industrialization that produced spectacular wealth on the surface while concealing profound inequality and corruption underneath. Between 1865 and 1900, the U.S. transformed from a largely agrarian nation into the world's leading industrial power. This happened through ruthless consolidation: Carnegie built a steel empire through vertical integration; Rockefeller controlled 90% of U.S. oil refining through horizontal integration and predatory pricing.

The winners celebrated Social Darwinism — the idea that wealth was a sign of natural superiority and that government regulation would only interfere with progress. The losers — workers, farmers, and immigrants — saw things differently. Labor unions (Knights of Labor, AFL) organized for better conditions, but strikes were broken by Pinkerton guards, state militia, and federal troops. Populists built a powerful third-party movement of farmers demanding silver coinage, railroad regulation, and progressive taxation.

For African Americans, the period was an unmitigated disaster. Plessy v. Ferguson (1896) enshrined "separate but equal" as constitutional doctrine. Lynching peaked in the 1890s. Two competing responses emerged: Booker T. Washington's strategy of economic self-improvement and accommodation vs. W.E.B. Du Bois's demand for immediate civil and political rights. This debate echoes through the entire Civil Rights era.

Native Americans faced the final destruction of their way of life: the buffalo was hunted to near-extinction (destroying Plains cultures), the Dawes Act (1887) broke up tribal lands, and Wounded Knee (1890) marked the effective end of armed resistance. The "closing of the frontier" (Turner Thesis, 1893) prompted Americans to look outward — toward empire.

The Big Picture

Period 7 spans half a century of massive change — from the Progressive response to Gilded Age inequality, through two world wars and the Great Depression. The through-line is the question of what role the federal government should play in American life — a question answered differently in each decade.

Progressivism (1900s–1920) was a middle-class response to the chaos of industrialization. Muckrakers exposed corruption; reformers demanded regulation. TR busted trusts, added national parks, and established the FDA. The 16th Amendment (income tax) and 17th (direct election of senators) transformed the constitutional structure. But Progressivism had a dark side: many Progressives supported eugenics, Jim Crow, and immigration restriction. It was reform for (middle-class, white) Americans, not all Americans.

The 1920s were a decade of contradiction: economic prosperity and consumer culture (Ford's Model T, radio, movies) alongside fierce cultural conflict (Prohibition, Scopes Trial, KKK revival, nativism). The Harlem Renaissance flourished. Immigration was sharply restricted. Women voted for the first time nationally. And beneath the prosperity, dangerous financial speculation and agricultural depression built toward catastrophe.

The Great Depression began in 1929 and destroyed the lives of millions. Hoover's limited response failed. FDR's New Deal used the federal government as a direct economic actor — relief (CCC, FERA), recovery (AAA, NRA), and reform (FDIC, SEC, Social Security, Wagner Act). The New Deal didn't end the Depression (WWII did), but it permanently expanded the federal government's role in American economic life.

WWII was simultaneously a triumph and a test of American values. The U.S. fought fascism abroad while maintaining racial segregation at home. Japanese American internment, the Double V Campaign (victory over fascism abroad AND racism at home), and the atomic bomb all raised profound moral questions about American identity.

Key Vocabulary

Muckrakers

Progressive Era investigative journalists who exposed corruption, dangerous conditions, and injustice. Key examples: Sinclair (The Jungle), Tarbell (Standard Oil), Riis (How the Other Half Lives).

Square Deal

TR's domestic program: "three C's" — control of corporations, consumer protection, conservation of natural resources. First use of federal power to regulate big business in the public interest.

Isolationism

Foreign policy position: U.S. should stay out of European conflicts (echoing Washington's Farewell Address). Dominant in 1930s until Pearl Harbor; revived after Vietnam and post-Cold War.

Keynesian Economics

Economic theory (John Maynard Keynes) that government should spend (deficit if necessary) during recessions to stimulate demand. Theoretical basis for New Deal; became mainstream after WWII.

Great Migration

Movement of ~6 million Black Americans from the South to Northern and Midwestern cities (1910–1970), driven by Jim Crow violence and economic opportunity in Northern factories.

Prohibition (18th Amendment)

1920–1933 ban on manufacture and sale of alcohol. Intended to reduce crime and poverty; instead created organized crime (bootlegging), speakeasies, and widespread law-breaking. Repealed by 21st Amendment.

New Deal Coalition

FDR's political alliance: urban workers, labor unions, ethnic immigrants, Southern whites, and Black voters (in the North). Dominated Democratic Party until 1960s–70s when Civil Rights Act split it.

The Big Picture

Period 8 is defined by the Cold War — not just as a foreign policy matter, but as a lens that shaped domestic politics, culture, and civil rights. The U.S. and USSR never fought directly, but competed for global influence through proxy wars, arms races, and ideological competition. The irony: America's most visible global mission was defending freedom and democracy while maintaining racial apartheid at home.

The Civil Rights Movement is the central domestic story. It was not a sudden explosion but a long campaign built on legal strategies (NAACP's litigation culminating in Brown), direct action (Montgomery, sit-ins, Freedom Rides), legislative lobbying, and massive organizing. The movement transformed American law (Civil Rights Act 1964, Voting Rights Act 1965) but also exposed deep divisions about how far that transformation would go — housing, economic equality, police violence.

LBJ's Great Society (1964–68) was the most ambitious domestic program since the New Deal: Medicare, Medicaid, federal education funding, the Voting Rights Act, and immigration reform. But Vietnam swallowed it. The Tet Offensive (1968) shattered public confidence; LBJ didn't run for reelection; RFK and MLK were assassinated. 1968 was the year America seemed to come apart.

Nixon's presidency mixed foreign policy achievements (détente, China) with the Watergate scandal that forced his resignation — the only presidential resignation in U.S. history. The 1970s brought stagflation, the oil crisis, and a profound loss of faith in institutions. By 1980, Americans were ready for Reagan's optimistic "Morning in America" message — and the conservative revolution that followed.

Key Vocabulary

Containment

Kennan's doctrine: don't roll back communism, but prevent it from spreading further. Implemented via Truman Doctrine, NATO, Korean War, Vietnam. Shaped all Cold War foreign policy.

McCarthyism

Senator McCarthy's reckless accusations of communist infiltration (1950–54). HUAC investigations; Hollywood blacklists; Rosenberg executions. Ended when McCarthy attacked the Army; "Have you no sense of decency?"

Domino Theory

If one country falls to communism, neighbors will follow "like dominoes." Used to justify Vietnam intervention. Largely disproven — Vietnam fell but Southeast Asia didn't go communist en masse.

Credibility Gap

Growing public distrust of government claims about Vietnam, particularly after Tet showed the war was not being won as officials claimed. Pentagon Papers (1971) confirmed systematic deception.

Great Society

LBJ's 1964–68 domestic program: Medicare, Medicaid, elementary education funding, Voting Rights Act, immigration reform, War on Poverty. Most ambitious expansion of federal programs since New Deal.

Détente

Nixon/Kissinger's policy of easing Cold War tensions through diplomacy rather than confrontation. SALT I arms control, China opening. Pragmatic approach from former anti-communist hawk.

Stagflation

1970s combination of high inflation AND high unemployment — previously thought impossible under Keynesian theory. Caused by oil embargoes and loose monetary policy. Discredited liberal economic management.

Military-Industrial Complex

Eisenhower's Farewell Address (1961) warning about the dangerous alliance between the defense industry, military, and government — that its influence could distort national priorities and threaten democracy.

The Big Picture

Reagan's 1980 election represented a fundamental realignment in American politics — a conservative revolution against the liberal consensus that had dominated since FDR. Reagan's message was simple and powerful: government is the problem, not the solution. His program (supply-side tax cuts, deregulation, military buildup, anti-union stance) reshaped the economic and political landscape for decades.

The Cold War ended not with a bang but with a gradual collapse. Gorbachev's reforms unleashed forces the Soviet system couldn't control; the Berlin Wall fell in 1989; the USSR dissolved in 1991. The "end of history" seemed to arrive — liberal democracy had triumphed. But new challenges quickly emerged: the Gulf War (1991), the Balkans, and then the seismic shock of September 11, 2001, which launched two decades of war in Afghanistan and Iraq and sparked profound debates about civil liberties, torture, and American power.

Globalization transformed the economy: NAFTA (1994) accelerated trade; manufacturing moved overseas; the technology revolution created new wealth but also new inequalities. The Rust Belt deindustrialized; the Sun Belt boomed. Immigration reshaped the demographic map. The 2008 financial crisis — the worst since the Depression — exposed the risks of financial deregulation and triggered the Tea Party backlash and a new era of populist politics.

The period since 1980 is characterized by deep political polarization — the parties sorted ideologically (moderate Republicans and Democrats largely disappeared), culture war politics intensified, and trust in institutions continued to decline. These trends all connect back to the unresolved tensions running through all of APUSH: race, inequality, immigration, federal power, and America's role in the world.

"Reaganomics" / "trickle-down": cut taxes for wealthy and corporations → investment → growth benefits everyone. Critics argue it increased inequality and deficits. Supporters credit 1980s expansion.

Reagan policy of removing government rules on industries (airlines, banking, telecommunications). Intended to spur competition and efficiency; contributed to 1980s Savings & Loan crisis and 2008 financial crisis.

Conservative political coalition combining economic conservatives (low taxes, free markets), social conservatives (anti-abortion, traditional family values), and neoconservatives (assertive foreign policy). Reagan's electoral base.

Integration of world economies through trade, capital, and labor flows. Created growth and consumer benefits, but also deindustrialization of U.S. manufacturing regions and wage pressure on working class.

Post-9/11 framework for U.S. foreign and domestic policy. Military campaigns in Afghanistan and Iraq; expanded surveillance; "enhanced interrogation"; debates about civil liberties vs. security still unresolved.

DBQ Strategy (Document-Based Question)

The DBQ is worth 25% of your exam score. You have 60 minutes (15 min reading + 45 min writing). You'll receive 7 documents and must write a historical argument essay using them.

The DBQ Rubric (7 points)

Point 1: Thesis/Claim (1 pt)

  • Must respond to the prompt with a historically defensible thesis
  • Must make a claim that establishes a line of reasoning (not just restate the prompt)
  • Can appear in the intro or conclusion
  • Formula: "Although [acknowledge complexity/counterargument], [your claim] because [reason 1], [reason 2], and [reason 3]."

Points 2–3: Contextualization + Evidence (2 pts)

  • Contextualization (1 pt): Describe a broader historical context that is relevant to the prompt. Must be MORE than just a phrase — at least 3-4 sentences explaining a development from BEFORE the time period and connecting it to your argument
  • Document Content (1 pt): Use content from at least 3 documents to address the topic

Points 4–5: Document Analysis (2 pts)

  • Evidence (1 pt): Use content from at least 6 of 7 documents to support your argument
  • Sourcing (1 pt): For at least 3 docs, explain the document's Historical Situation, Audience, Purpose, OR Point of View — and explain HOW it affects the argument

Points 6–7: Complexity (2 pts)

  • Outside Evidence (1 pt): Use relevant evidence not found in the documents
  • Complexity (1 pt): Demonstrate a complex understanding — corroborate, qualify, OR modify the argument using evidence of: nuance across time, different perspectives, multiple causes/effects, or connections across time periods
HAPP Sourcing FormulaDon't just identify the source — explain WHY it matters. "Because the author is a Southern plantation owner writing in 1850 (Historical Situation), he downplays the brutality of slavery to defend his economic interests (Purpose), which means we should read his claim about 'benevolent masters' skeptically (Point of View)."

DBQ Time Management

Minutes 1–15

Read & Annotate

Read each doc; jot: HAPP (Historical Situation, Audience, Purpose, POV) + main argument; group docs by theme

Minutes 15–20

Outline

Write thesis; plan 2-3 body paragraphs; decide which docs go where; note your outside evidence

Minutes 20–55

Write

Intro (contextualization + thesis) → body paragraphs → conclusion (restate + complexity if not woven in)

Minutes 55–60

Review

Check: thesis clear? 6+ docs used? 3+ sourcing? Outside evidence? Contextualization paragraph?

LEQ & SAQ Strategy

The LEQ (Long Essay Question) is worth 15% of your score — 40 minutes, no documents, pure knowledge. The SAQ (Short Answer Question) is worth 20% — 3 questions, 40 minutes total, brief targeted responses.

LEQ Rubric (6 points)

Same structure as DBQ, minus Document points

  • Thesis (1 pt): Historically defensible claim with line of reasoning
  • Contextualization (1 pt): Broader context — at least a paragraph, connected to argument
  • Evidence (2 pts): Specific evidence (1 pt) + uses evidence to support argument (1 pt)
  • Historical Reasoning (1 pt): Uses causation, CCOT, or comparison to frame argument
  • Complexity (1 pt): Corroborate, qualify, or modify with nuance across time/perspective
LEQ Thesis Formula"While [acknowledge complexity], [your argument] because [cause/factor 1], [cause/factor 2], and [cause/factor 3]." Each body paragraph = one reason. Use specific historical evidence (dates, names, events) in every paragraph.

SAQ Strategy

SAQ Format: Always answers A, B, C

  • Each part is worth 1 point (3 pts per SAQ)
  • No thesis required — but be specific
  • 2-4 sentences per part is usually enough
  • Part A: Usually "describe" or "explain" a specific development
  • Part B: Usually "explain a cause" or "explain an effect"
  • Part C: Usually "explain how [X] changed or continued" — requires CCOT thinking
SAQ Tip: Always name-dropVague answers don't earn points. "Farmers were angry" → 0 pts. "Southern and Western farmers organized the Populist Party in 1892 to demand free silver coinage and government ownership of railroads in response to falling crop prices and railroad monopolies" → full credit.

Historical Reasoning Skills for Essays

Causation

Why did X happen?

Identify multiple causes. Distinguish immediate causes from long-term/structural ones. Explain consequences too.

CCOT

Continuity & Change Over Time

What changed? What stayed the same? What caused the change? Anchor analysis at beginning AND end of period.

Comparison

How are X and Y similar/different?

Structure: point-by-point OR block comparison. Always explain significance of the similarities/differences.

Contextualization

Broader context

Describe a development from outside/before your topic and explain how it connects to your argument. Worth 1 point on every essay.

Mixed Practice

These questions span multiple periods and test your ability to make connections across time — a key skill on the AP exam.
1
MediumContinuity & Change
Which of the following best represents a continuity in American history from the colonial era through the late 19th century?
Answer: B
Nativism persisted from colonial-era anti-Catholic sentiment → anti-Irish sentiment in the 1840s-50s → Chinese Exclusion Act (1882) → Immigration Restriction League and quotas of 1924. This continuity of nativist fear and exclusion toward immigrants is a major APUSH theme (MIG). All other options describe changes, not continuities.
2
HardComparison
A historian comparing the First Great Awakening (1730s–40s) with the Second Great Awakening (1790s–1840s) would most likely argue that both
Answer: B
Both Awakenings shared a common pattern: emotional, accessible preaching that challenged established church authority and democratic-ized religion. The 1st GA contributed to democratic thinking that fed Revolutionary ideology. The 2nd GA directly fueled reform movements (abolitionism, temperance, women's rights). Religious enthusiasm translated into social activism in both cases.
3
MediumCausation
Which of the following most accurately describes a long-term cause of the Civil War?
Answer: C
The long-term, structural cause of the Civil War was the fundamental economic divergence between North and South — and the incompatibility of free-labor industrialism with chattel slavery. A, B, and D are immediate/proximate triggers, not long-term causes. The economic and social systems of the two regions made conflict increasingly inevitable throughout the antebellum period.
4
HardContextualization
An APUSH essay about the New Deal would earn the contextualization point by discussing
Answer: B
Contextualization requires a broader historical development from OUTSIDE or BEFORE your topic that connects to your argument. A, C, and D are all directly about the New Deal itself. 5
MediumCross-Period
The concept of "containment," articulated by George Kennan in 1946, was most similar in its logic to which earlier American foreign policy?
Answer: A
The Monroe Doctrine (1823) declared that European powers should not extend their influence into the Western Hemisphere — essentially containing European expansion. Kennan's containment aimed to prevent Soviet communism from spreading further. Both policies define a sphere of interest and resist a rival power's expansion, though through very different means and global contexts.
6
HardComparison: Reform Movements
Which of the following most accurately compares Progressivism (1900–1920) with the New Deal (1933–1939)?
Answer: A
Progressives regulated capitalism — breaking up trusts, establishing the FDA, creating the Federal Reserve — but left the market fundamentally intact. The New Deal went further: the federal government directly employed millions (CCC, WPA), insured bank deposits (FDIC), guaranteed collective bargaining (Wagner Act), and created Social Security. Both preserved capitalism, but the New Deal vastly expanded the state's role as economic actor and safety net provider.
7
MediumFederal Power Expansion
Which sequence correctly orders these expansions of federal power from most limited to most expansive?
Answer: C
This CCOT sequence traces federal power's growth: the Articles gave the central government almost no power. Hamilton's program (national bank, assumption of debts) established the principle of active federal economic management. The New Deal created a social safety net and regulatory state. The Great Society added Medicare, Medicaid, and education funding — the most extensive peacetime expansion of federal programs in U.S. history. This is a classic APUSH change-over-time arc.
8
HardComparison: Racial Discrimination
Which statement best describes the relationship between the end of Reconstruction (1877) and the Civil Rights Movement (1950s–1960s)?
Answer: A
The 13th, 14th, and 15th Amendments promised equality that was immediately subverted by Black Codes, sharecropping, Jim Crow, and Supreme Court rulings (Plessy, Cruikshank). The Civil Rights Movement — through Brown (invoking the 14th Amendment), the Civil Rights Act of 1964 (protecting 14th Amendment rights), and the Voting Rights Act of 1965 (enforcing the 15th Amendment) — completed what Reconstruction had begun. This CCOT arc is central to APUSH.
